Choosing metadata fields for an archive about walls presents a unique challenge, particularly within the framework of media archaeology. Walls, both physical and conceptual, serve as mediums that shape communication, access, and power dynamics. Metadata fields for such an archive must navigate the interplay between materiality and meaning—capturing the wall as both an object and a social construct.

In media archaeology, the wall is not merely a static barrier but a participant in communication systems, mediating who can see, hear, or access information. For instance, a field like “Material Composition” might be necessary to contextualize a wall’s durability or transparency, while “Temporal Context” could situate it within specific technological or cultural developments. The challenge lies in creating fields that respect the wall’s multifaceted nature without oversimplifying its role in shaping human interaction.

Moreover, walls influence how and with whom we communicate. Fields such as “Function” (e.g., boundary, screen, memorial) and “Audience Impact” could capture the sociopolitical and emotional layers of walls. The difficulty arises in ensuring these fields reflect not just the wall’s historical role but also its speculative potential in shaping future media environments. Ultimately, this process demands a balance between precision and openness to interpretation, fostering rich, multidimensional engagement.

 Basic Information

	•	Title: A descriptive or given title for the image (e.g., “Brick Wall with Graffiti”).
	•	Date: Date the image was captured or created.
	•	Location: Geographical location of the wall (city, country, GPS coordinates).
	•	Creator/Photographer: Name of the person or entity that captured or created the image.
	•	Medium: Type of wall (e.g., brick, concrete, glass, interactive display).
	
	2. Contextual Information

	•	Historical Context: Information about the wall’s time period, construction, or historical significance.
	•	Cultural Significance: The role of the wall in local or broader cultural practices (e.g., a Berlin Wall segment or a mural depicting social movements).
	•	Function of the Wall: The purpose of the wall (e.g., barrier, billboard, projection screen, support for public art).
	
	3. Accessibility and Interaction

	•	Accessibility Features: Any design elements that make the wall accessible (e.g., ramps, tactile surfaces, braille signage).
	•	Interaction Mechanisms: How people interact with the wall (e.g., touchscreens, graffiti, projection mapping).
	•	Barriers to Accessibility: Elements that limit access or interaction (e.g., height, surveillance, restrictive signage).
	
	4. Surveillance and Power

	•	Surveillance Elements: Presence of cameras, motion sensors, or other monitoring devices on or near the wall.
	•	Power Dynamics: How the wall reinforces or resists power structures (e.g., as a surveillance barrier or a site of protest).
	•	Visibility: Whether the wall obscures or enhances visibility (e.g., transparent glass vs. opaque concrete).
	
	5. Media and Communication

	•	Media Content: Types of media displayed on or related to the wall (e.g., advertisements, murals, digital projections).
	•	Role in Mediation: How the wall shapes media consumption or understanding (e.g., framing advertisements, supporting street art).
	•	Information Conveyed: Messages communicated by the wall, whether intentional (e.g., signage) or interpretive (e.g., graffiti).
	•	Technology Embedded: Integration of technology, such as screens, augmented reality, or audio systems.
	
	6. Behavioral and Social Dynamics

	•	Behavioral Influence: How the wall shapes behavior (e.g., directing pedestrian traffic, discouraging trespassing).
	•	Social Interaction: How people engage with the wall or with others around it (e.g., as a meeting place, protest site).
	•	Perceived Role: Public perception of the wall (e.g., protective, oppressive, artistic).
	
	7. Aesthetic and Material Details

	•	Material: Composition of the wall (e.g., brick, steel, wood).
	•	Condition: Physical state of the wall (e.g., intact, deteriorating, vandalized).
	•	Dimensions: Height, width, and scale of the wall.
	•	Color/Texture: Visual and tactile characteristics.
	
	8. Metadata for Research and Use

	•	Keywords/Tags: Relevant terms for easy retrieval (e.g., “surveillance,” “urban barriers,” “media consumption”).
	•	Rights and Permissions: Copyright or usage restrictions for the image.
	•	Related Media: Links to associated content (e.g., videos, articles, or related images).
	•	Research Notes: Observations or insights relevant to the archive’s themes.
	
	9. Interpretative Themes

	•	Archaeological Significance: Relevance of the wall to media archaeology or historical narratives.
	•	Narrative Potential: Stories or meanings associated with the wall.
	•	Symbolism: Symbolic associations of the wall within its context (e.g., freedom, oppression, separation).
