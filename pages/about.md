The creation of this archive has been inspired by my relationship with a series of walls and fences in Los Angeles. Access to physical spaces is always a mediated process but thinking more and more about how we feel and perceive our interactions with walls helps to broaden our understanding of larger systems and complex societal structures. In particular, the aim of this archive is to better understand how walls and other similar structures might be understood within the context of media archaeology and media studies more broadly. 

On the surface most physical walls or fences have little to animate or activate them in the same way that media objects can be understood. There are of course digital media walls and other such artifacts that can be easily understood within a traditional media framework. The goal here though is to make linkages between the functionality of walls and how our attitudes, beliefs, thoughts, actions and so forth can be impacted by our experience of living alongside and with these structures. More importantly, how do they impact and shape what we say and think about on topics such as access, privacy, ethics, and more. 

In this archive of the everyday I see something potentially powerful and profound that marks each and every one of us.

**Media Archaeology and Walls and Communication**

Approaches in media archaeology have called for a more holistic and interconnected understanding of media histories and objects. One aspect about walls and other barries is trying to understand what they communicate.

1. Walls immediately give information to you within an environment about space.
2. Walls physically prevent or mediate access.

What can we learn by studying more closely the communicative elements of structures such as walls?
