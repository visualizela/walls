**The everyday experience of walls**

Walls, fences, and barriers are everywhere. How we interact with them and how they impact our day-to-day lives is remarkable. Embedded within our media landscape and environment, these structures serve not only as physical dividers but also as mediators of perception, behavior, and communication. They shape how we move, what we see, and even what we believe. Whether separating spaces, controlling access, or acting as canvases for expression, these boundaries profoundly influence our social and cultural dynamics.

**Archive**
Creating an archive of examples of these structures is both important and necessary. By systematically documenting and analyzing walls and barriers, we gain deeper insights into their roles as media objects—tools that not only restrict or protect but also convey messages, enforce ideologies, and shape interactions. This archive can reveal the ways in which walls contribute to larger narratives of power, accessibility, and surveillance, helping us understand their significance in past and present contexts.

Through such an archive, we can trace how walls evolve in function and meaning, from ancient fortifications to modern border fences, from urban graffiti walls to corporate branding installations. It allows us to interrogate how these structures mediate relationships between individuals, communities, and institutions. Furthermore, it provides a foundation for critically assessing how walls reinforce or challenge issues like inclusion, exclusion, and control in both physical and digital spaces.

This effort becomes even more crucial as we navigate a world increasingly defined by visible and invisible barriers. An archive serves as a repository for reflection, critique, and potential reimagination, enabling us to better understand how these seemingly inert structures actively shape our media consumption, societal norms, and lived experiences.
